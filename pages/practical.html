Practical Self-Care

Practical self-care means anything that supports all the other areas of your life, they are the tasks you complete that fulfill core aspects of your life in order to prevent future stressful situations.

Planning Ahead

Why plan ahead?

  1. Helps you focus on your priorities and be more productive.
  2. Helps you say 'No'.
  3. Helps you to feel in control.
  4. Helps increase your accountability levels.
  5. Helps you eliminate interruptions.

How to plan the week ahead?

  1. Plan you non-negotiables.
  2. Plan your most important tasks.
  3. Make time for downtime and sleep.
  4. Then plan everything else.
  5. Make sure your plan is achievable!

Adopt the "Getting Things Done" framework

  1. Capture all the things! Your ideas, recurring tasks, long emails, responses, meetings, everything. Plan your most important things.
  2. Clarify the things you have to do. This is the time to decide if that task needs action or not. Plan everything else.
  3. Organize those action tasks by priority and category. Assign due dates when possible.
  4. Reflect on your to-do list. This is when you revise all the system and pick what your task should be.
  5. Engage and get to work. At this stage, all your tasks are organized by priorities and broken down into actions. You know exactly what you should be doing.

Organizing your Space

Organization Saves Time, Money, and Other Resources

  1. A messy, disorganized home can cost you more than just your inner peace. If you don’t have a ‘home’ for all of your belongings, you spend more time trying to put things away when you’re cleaning up, and waste time looking for items when you need them.

Remember the 3 R's

  1. Reduce what you have. It is the most direct path to efficient organizing.
  2. Be resourceful. When you have less, you find more creative ways to use your belongings.
  3. Be resilient. If you find you do not have something you need, don't get bent out of shape or rush out to buy more.

Take 3 Minutes Each Day

  1. There is no organizational system in the world that will work if it's not maintained. Aim for a system, or level of belongings, that will let you pick up any room in three minutes. Then, after dinner, have the family pitch in with clean up. Before sitting down for TV or relaxation time, walk around and put everything away so you're not leaving it until just before bed when you're too tired to move.

Budgeting Tips

Unfortunately, the word budget has gotten a bad rap. When it all boils down, a budget is basically just a plan for your money. Budgeting means you’re spending with purpose before the month begins. But many people view a budget as a straitjacket that will keep them from doing what they want. But that couldn’t be further from the truth! A budget doesn’t limit your freedom, it gives you freedom! It’s really all about being intentional with where your money goes.

Pick the budgeting method that's right for you.

Zero-based budgeting is a way of budgeting where your income minus your expenses equals zero. With a zero-based budget, you have to make sure your expenses match your income during the month. That way you’re giving every dollar that’s coming in a job to do. Here are the basic steps:

  1. 1. Write down your monthly income.
  2. 2. Write down your monthly expenses.
  3. 3. Write down your seasonal expenses.
  4. 4. Subtract your income from your expenses to equal zero.

The 50/30/20 rule budget is a simple way to budget that doesn’t involve detailed budgeting categories. It only requires you to track and divide your expenses into three main categories: needs, wants, and savings or debt. This reduces the amount of time you have to spend detailing your finances and allows you to focus more on the big picture instead.

  1. 50%: Needs, they are those bills that you absolutely must pay and are the things necessary for survival. Half of your after-tax income should be all that you need to cover your needs and obligations. If you are spending more than that on your needs, you will have to either cut down on wants or try to downsize your lifestyle, perhaps to a smaller home or more modest car or cooking at home more often.
  2. 30%: Wants, these are all the things you spend money on that are not absolutely essential. This category also includes those upgrade decisions you make, such as choosing a costlier steak instead of a less expensive hamburger, buying a Mercedes instead of a more economical Honda, or spending money to watch cable TV. Basically, wants are all those little extras you spend money on that makes life more enjoyable and entertaining.
  3. 20%: Savings, this includes adding money to an emergency fund in a bank savings account, making IRA contributions to a mutual fund account, and investing in the stock market. Savings can also include debt repayment. While minimum payments are part of the "needs" category, any extra payments reduce the principal and future interest owed, so they are savings.
